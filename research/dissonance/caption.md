# Figure Caption: Musical Consonance from Frequency Interactions

**How musical consonance emerges from the interaction of harmonic spectra and auditory physiology.** (A) Sensory roughness for two pure tones at 200 Hz and 200·r Hz as a function of frequency ratio r, based on the Plomp–Levelt model. Roughness peaks when frequencies fall within the same critical band. (B) Total dissonance for musical intervals, computed by summing roughness contributions from all partial–partial interactions (30 harmonics). Deep minima occur at simple frequency ratios (octave 2:1, perfect fifth 3:2, perfect fourth 4:3, major third 5:4), corresponding to consonant intervals. (C) Kernel density estimates showing the distribution of frequency differences (Δf) between all pairs of partials for each interval, displayed within 0–200 Hz (one octave). Vertical dotted lines mark fundamental frequency differences; black dashed curve shows ear roughness sensitivity from Panel A for reference. Consonance emerges not from simple frequency ratios alone, but from how harmonic partial interactions distribute relative to auditory sensitivity. Model uses f₀ = 200 Hz, constant critical bandwidth of 100 Hz, and equal-amplitude partials.
